Basic structural and functional unit of all living organisms.

Analogy: A cell is like a tiny factory — each part does a specific job, all work together.

Microscopy — how we see cells
👁
Unaided eye
Limit of resolution = 0.1 mm. Dots closer than this appear as one.
Most cells (10–100 µm) — invisible. Chicken egg is visible.
Like trying to read tiny print — your eye blurs it into one blob.
🔬
Light microscope
Robert Hooke (1665) — first to observe cells in cork. Named them "cells" (Latin: small rooms).
Total magnification = objective × eyepiece. E.g. 10× × 10× = 100×
Cell size = field diameter ÷ number of cells along diameter.
Like a camera zoom — the more you zoom in, the finer the detail.
⚡
Electron microscope
Uses electrons instead of light — resolves at nanometre scale.
Reveals ribosomes (10 nm), viruses, proteins, fine membrane details.
1 nm = 0.000001 mm. Far beyond what light can resolve.
Like going from a blurry photo to 4K — completely different level of detail.
Cell membrane — the gatekeeper
M
Fluid-mosaic model
Thickness: 7–10 nm. Made of lipids + proteins.
Lipid bilayer — two layers of fat molecules. Heads outward (love water), tails inward (hate water).
Proteins float in the bilayer like icebergs — act as channels (gatekeepers).
Molecules move sideways → "fluid". Tiled pattern → "mosaic".
Selectively permeable — lets water & O₂/CO₂ pass; blocks large molecules.
Like a security door — opens for authorised molecules, blocks others.
O
Osmosis — water movement
Diffusion — particles move high → low concentration. No membrane needed. E.g. perfume spreading in a room.
Osmosis — water moves high → low concentration across a selectively permeable membrane.
Water always moves from dilute (more water) to concentrated (less water) solution.
Potato experiment (Activity 2.2)
Three types of solutions
Isotonic
Same conc. inside & outside → no change
Hypotonic
Outside weaker → water in → cell swells
Hypertonic
Outside stronger → water out → cell shrinks
Why chips become soggy when kept in water — osmosis! Why pickles preserve food — hypertonic salt draws water out of bacteria → they die.
Cell wall — the rigid outer armour
W
Cell wall
Present in plants, fungi, bacteria. Absent in animals.
Made of cellulose (many glucose units linked). Cellulose in our diet = roughage → aids digestion.
Rigid yet permeable — water and dissolved minerals pass through freely.
Keeps plant firm, withstands wind, rain, gravity.
Like a brick wall around a building — gives shape and strength. The cell membrane inside is the actual door (selective).
Prokaryotic vs eukaryotic cells
P
Prokaryotic (pro = primitive)
No true nucleus — DNA in a region called nucleoid (no membrane around it).
No membrane-bound organelles. Size: 1–10 µm. Usually unicellular.
Has: cell wall, cell membrane, cytoplasm, ribosomes, flagellum (for movement).
Like a studio apartment — everything in one open room, no separate compartments.
Examples: Bacteria (E. coli, Thermophiles of Puga Valley Ladakh), Blue-green algae.
E
Eukaryotic (eu = true)
True nucleus with double-membrane nuclear envelope. Size: 10–100 µm.
Has membrane-bound organelles — each is a separate compartment.
Cytoskeleton — fine fibres for support and internal transport.
Unicellular (yeast, Amoeba) or multicellular (plants, animals, fungi).
Like a multi-room office building — each department (organelle) has its own room.
Interesting: Mature RBCs
Human red blood cells have no nucleus — more space for haemoglobin → more O₂ carried. Lifespan only ~120 days as they can't repair or divide.
Examples: Onion cells, cheek cells, yeast, Amoeba, leaf cells.

Cell organelles — the cell is like a tiny factory
Nucleus — Control centre / CEO of the cell
House of coded instructions
Double nuclear membrane with pores (allow material exchange). Nucleolus inside — makes ribosome subunits. Contains chromosomes (DNA + proteins). Chromatin = loose form of DNA in non-dividing cell. Genes = functional segments of DNA that carry traits.
Like the head office of a company — all important decisions and instructions come from here.
Ribosomes — Protein factories
Smallest organelle; no membrane
Free in cytoplasm or attached to RER surface. Site of protein synthesis. Present in ALL cells — prokaryotic and eukaryotic. Made in nucleolus (subunits), assembled in cytoplasm.
Like workers on an assembly line — they build proteins from instructions (mRNA).
Endoplasmic Reticulum (ER) — Manufacturing factory
Network of membranes; connected to nuclear envelope
RER (Rough ER) — ribosomes on surface → looks rough. Makes proteins for export (e.g. insulin in pancreatic cells, digestive enzymes). SER (Smooth ER) — no ribosomes. Makes lipids, steroid hormones (sex hormones), stores Ca²⁺.
RER = the kitchen (cooks/makes proteins). SER = the storage room (stores fats, hormones).
Golgi apparatus — Post office / Packaging centre
Stacks of flattened sacs (cisternae)
Receives proteins/lipids from ER in vesicles, modifies them, packages into vesicles and sends to cell membrane (secretion), lysosomes, or other destinations. First seen by Camillo Golgi (1898) in barn owl nerve cells using special staining.
Like Amazon's sorting warehouse — receives goods, stamps labels, ships to correct address.
Lysosomes — Clean-up crew / Suicide bags
Single membrane; filled with digestive enzymes
Break down worn-out organelles, foreign particles, waste molecules (proteins, carbs, fats). Products reused in cytoplasm. Keep the cell clean and healthy. Sperm cells use lysosomal enzymes to break the outer layer of the egg during fertilisation.
Like the garbage disposal system + janitor of the cell — destroys waste, recycles materials.
Mitochondria — Powerhouse of the cell
Double membrane; has own DNA and ribosomes
Outer membrane smooth and porous. Inner membrane folded into cristae (finger-like) → increases surface area for ATP-making reactions. Cellular respiration: glucose → ATP (energy currency). Has own DNA → shares evolutionary history with bacteria.
Like the power plant of a city — burns fuel (glucose) to produce electricity (ATP) for everything else.
Vacuoles — Storage tanks
Membrane-bound storage organelle
Plant cells: ONE large central vacuole filled with cell sap (water, minerals, sugars, waste). Maintains turgor pressure → keeps plant firm and upright. Less water → vacuole shrinks → plant wilts. Animal cells: small, temporary vacuoles for material storage.
Like a water tank on a building rooftop — stores supplies and maintains pressure throughout the structure.
Cytoplasm — The factory floor
Semi-fluid, jelly-like, fills the cell
Surrounds and suspends all organelles. In prokaryotes, ALL cellular activities happen here (no organelles). May contain cell inclusions: starch granules (plant cells), crystals of calcium oxalate or silica. Cytoskeleton (fine fibres) provides shape and support (visible only under electron microscope).
Like the factory floor — everything is set up on it and all activity happens within it.

Plastids — plant cells only (3 types)
🌿
Chloroplasts
Double membrane. Green pigment chlorophyll absorbs sunlight → photosynthesis.
Inside: stroma (semi-fluid) + thylakoid discs (contain chlorophyll).
Sugars made + starch stored in stroma. Has own DNA & ribosomes.
Found in all green parts — leaves, unripe fruits, green stems.
🌸
Chromoplasts
Contain yellow, orange, or red pigments (not chlorophyll).
Chroma (Greek) = colour.
Found in petals and ripe fruits — bright colours attract pollinators and seed-dispersers.
Tomatoes red, marigolds orange, lemons yellow — all due to chromoplasts! Ripe bananas turn from green (chloroplast) to yellow (chromoplast).
⬜
Leucoplasts
Colourless — no pigment. Leukos (Greek) = white.
Store food: starch, oils, or proteins.
Found in roots, seeds, and non-green storage organs.
Potato and Colocasia (taro/arbi) store starch in leucoplasts — that is why they are starchy! Seeds store oils in leucoplasts (mustard, sunflower).
Cell division — mitosis vs meiosis
M
Mitosis — growth & repair
1 parent cell → 2 daughter cells identical to parent (same chromosome number, same DNA).
For: growth, repair, replacement of dead cells, asexual reproduction.
Errors → uncontrolled division → tumours.
Every day ~100 billion cells replaced in the human body (≈1% of total).
Like photocopying a document — you get exact copies of the original.
Mx
Meiosis — sexual reproduction
Only in reproductive organs (testes, ovaries, anthers).
Parent divides twice → 4 daughter cells (gametes) with HALF chromosomes.
Creates genetic diversity — children resemble parents but differ from each other.
Fertilisation: sperm (n=23) + egg (n=23) → zygote (2n=46) — chromosome number restored.
Like shuffling a deck of cards and dealing — each hand (gamete) is unique. No two siblings are exactly alike because of meiosis.
Cell theory & cell life cycle
📜
Cell theory — proposed by three scientists
Matthias Schleiden
German botanist · 1838
All plants are made of cells.
Theodor Schwann
German zoologist · 1839
All animals are also made of cells.
Rudolf Virchow
German scientist · 1855
New cells arise only from pre-existing cells.
All living organisms are made of one or more cells Cell is the basic unit of structure and function All cells arise from pre-existing cells
Contact inhibition & cancer
Contact inhibition — normal animal cells stop dividing when they touch neighbouring cells. Cancer cells lose this → uncontrolled division → tumours (benign or malignant) → may spread to other organs.
Programmed Cell Death (PCD) — cells die in a controlled, genetically regulated way. E.g. cells between fingers of a foetus are removed by PCD — without it, we'd have webbed hands.
Cell culture — cells grown outside the body in nutrient medium (used to make vaccines, medicines, study diseases).
