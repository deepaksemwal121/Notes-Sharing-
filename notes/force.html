6.1 — The push, the pull

What exactly is a force?

A force can start an object moving, stop it, speed it up, slow it down, change its direction, or change its shape. Every time we describe a force, we always mention which way it's pushing or pulling — direction matters just as much as strength.

6.4

Newton's First Law — the law of inertia

1

"An object at rest remains at rest, and an object in motion continues to move with a constant velocity, unless a net force acts upon it."

Isaac Newton used the word inertia for this tendency of objects to resist a change in their state of rest or motion. Interestingly, it was Galileo Galilei, over a century earlier, who first argued through thought experiments that a moving object needs no force to keep moving at constant velocity — only friction (which is everywhere in daily life) makes it seem otherwise.

🤔 Think about it: If the net force on an object is exactly zero, its position-time graph is either flat (at rest) or a straight sloped line (constant velocity) — never curved.
6.5

Newton's Second Law — force, mass & acceleration

2

"When a net force acts on an object, it accelerates in the direction of the net force. The acceleration is proportional to the net force and inversely proportional to the object's mass."

F = m × a
a = 2.00 m/s²

Defining the newton

1 N is the force needed to give a 1 kg object an acceleration of 1 m/s². So F = (1 kg)×(1 m/s²) = 1 N.

Weight = mg

The gravitational force on an object is F = mg, where g ≈ 9.8 m/s² near Earth's surface (often rounded to 10 m/s² for quick estimates).

6.6

Newton's Third Law — action & reaction

3

"Whenever one object exerts a force on a second object, the second object simultaneously exerts an equal and opposite force on the first."

These paired forces act on two different objects — so they never cancel each other out. Tap a card to see the pair.

🚀 Real mission: Chandrayaan-3's Vikram lander fired its engine in the direction of its own motion to slow itself down — the exhaust gases pushed back against the lander, enabling a soft landing near the Moon's south pole.
6.7 — Zooming out

Treating connected objects as one system

Two boxes tied by a string and pulled by a force F? Instead of analysing each box separately, treat them as a single system of combined mass. The internal tension force cancels out, leaving:

a = F ÷ (m₁ + m₂)
🚶 Everyday version: While walking, your arms and legs move in complicated ways — yet your overall motion can be studied by treating your whole body as one object. Physics gets simpler when you stop looking at the parts and look at the whole.
