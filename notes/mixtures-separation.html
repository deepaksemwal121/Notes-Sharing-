Is Matter Around Us Pure? + Exploring Mixtures

Matter

Anything that has mass and occupies space. Classified by chemical composition.

Pure substance Mixture Element Compound Homogeneous Heterogeneous
Pure substances
E
Element
Made of one kind of atom only. Cannot be broken down by chemical reactions.
Metals
Lustrous, malleable, ductile, conduct heat & electricity, sonorous. E.g. Gold, Silver, Copper, Iron, Sodium
Mercury is the only metal liquid at room temperature.
Non-metals
Poor conductors, not lustrous/malleable. E.g. Oxygen, Hydrogen, Iodine, Carbon, Bromine, Chlorine
Metalloids
Intermediate properties. E.g. Boron, Silicon, Germanium
92 naturally occurring elements; 11 gases at room temp; 2 liquids (Hg, Br).
C
Compound
Two or more elements chemically combined in a fixed ratio by mass.
Properties are totally different from constituent elements.
Separated only by chemical or electrochemical reactions — not physical methods.
Examples
Water (H₂O) H:O = 1:8 by mass always — fixed ratio
Salt (NaCl) Sodium chloride — table salt
Sugar (C₁₂H₂₂O₁₁) Sucrose — fixed composition
Iron sulphide (FeS) Formed when Fe + S heated together — smells like rotten eggs with HCl
Mixture of Fe + S: magnetic, Fe dissolves in CS₂. Compound FeS: not magnetic, H₂S gas with HCl.
Types of mixtures
S
Solution (homogeneous)
Uniform composition throughout — same taste in first & last sip of lemonade.
Solute + Solvent
Solute dissolved substance (less). E.g. sugar, salt
Solvent does the dissolving (more). E.g. water, alcohol
Properties
Particles <1 nm — not visible to naked eye
Does not scatter light — no Tyndall effect
Cannot be filtered; does not settle
examples
Sugar in water, salt in water, tincture of iodine (iodine in alcohol), soda water (CO₂ in water), air (gaseous solution), alloys (solid solution)
C
Colloid (heterogeneous)
Appears homogeneous but is actually heterogeneous. Particles 1–1000 nm.
Properties
Scatters light — Tyndall effect visible
Does not settle; cannot be filtered
Separated by centrifugation
Tyndall effect
Scattering of beam of light by colloid particles. Sunlight through forest canopy, floodlights in stadium.
examples
Milk, blood, fog, smoke, jelly, shaving cream, butter, ice cream, tomato sauce
Components
Dispersed phase + Dispersion medium
Su
Suspension (heterogeneous)
Solids dispersed in liquids but do not dissolve. Particles >1000 nm.
Properties
Visible to naked eye
Scatters light — Tyndall effect visible
Settles on standing — unstable
Can be separated by filtration
examples
Chalk + water, muddy water, flour in water, sand in water, sawdust in water
Coagulation (Ch 5)
Alum (fitkari) added to muddy water causes suspended particles to clump and settle. Lemon juice/vinegar coagulates milk to make paneer.

Concentration of a solution (Ch 2 + Ch 5)
%
Expressing concentration
Mass by mass % (% m/m)
Conc. = (Mass of solute / Mass of solution) × 100
E.g. 40g salt in 320g water → 40/360 × 100 = 11.1%
Mass by volume % (% m/v)
= (Mass of solute / Volume of solution) × 100
E.g. 5% glucose IV drip = 5g glucose per 100 mL solution
Volume by volume % (% v/v)
= (Volume of solute / Volume of solution) × 100
E.g. Vinegar = 5% v/v acetic acid; perfumes
S
Solubility
Max amount of solute that dissolves in fixed solvent at a given temperature.
Types of solutions
Saturated No more solute dissolves at given temp
Unsaturated Can dissolve more solute
Dilute Small amount of solute (comparative)
Concentrated Large amount of solute (comparative)
Effect of temperature
Solids in liquid: solubility increases with temperature.
Gases in liquid: solubility decreases with temperature.
Real example: 0.9% m/v NaCl saline drip used in hospitals — replaces lost fluids safely.
Separation techniques (Ch 2 · section 2.3 + Ch 5 · sections 5.3 & 5.4)
↕
Separation of homogeneous mixtures
Evaporation
Removes volatile solvent; non-volatile solute stays.
Ink on watch glass → dye stays, water evaporates
Crystallisation
Saturated solution cooled slowly → pure crystals form. Better than simple evaporation (sugar may char).
Copper sulphate crystals; sea salt purification; mishri (candy sugar)
Distillation
Separate miscible liquids with boiling point difference ≥25°C. Vapour → condenser → pure liquid.
Acetone (56°C) from water (100°C); Mitti ka Ittar — Kannauj, UP (Deg-Bhapka method)
Fractional distillation
Boiling point difference <25°C. Fractionating column of glass beads used.
Air → N₂ (−196°C), Ar (−186°C), O₂ (−183°C); crude petroleum refining → petrol, diesel, kerosene
Paper chromatography
Separates solutes dissolved in same solvent. Components travel at different speeds on paper. From Greek: chroma = colour.
Dyes in black ink, pigments from spinach leaves, drugs from blood
↕
Separation of heterogeneous mixtures
Separating funnel
Immiscible liquids form separate layers by density. Lower layer drained through stopcock.
Kerosene/mustard oil above water; iron extraction — slag removed from molten iron
Sublimation
Solid → vapour directly (below melting point), bypassing liquid. Vapour cools → deposition.
Ammonium chloride + salt ; camphor + sand; naphthalene; dry ice (CO₂)
Filtration
Insoluble solid separated from liquid using filter paper.
Mud from water; removing impurities before crystallisation
Magnetic separation
Magnetic material attracted to magnet; non-magnetic stays.
Iron filings from sulphur powder
Coagulation
Coagulant causes fine suspended particles to clump together → settle (sedimentation) → filtered.
Alum (fitkari) in muddy water; lemon juice/vinegar to make paneer from milk
Centrifugation
High-speed spinning — denser particles to bottom, lighter on top.
Cream from milk; blood components (plasma, RBC, WBC); urine tests; washing machines
Physical vs chemical changes
P
Physical change
No change in chemical composition or nature of substance.
Interconversion of states: ice ↔ water ↔ steam — chemically identical throughout.
Properties changed: colour, shape, size — not chemical nature.
Examples
Melting butter, boiling water, dissolving salt in water, making fruit salad, cutting trees, mixing iron filings + sand
C
Chemical change (reaction)
New substance(s) formed with different chemical properties. Also called a chemical reaction.
One substance reacts with another — change in chemical composition.
Examples
Rusting of iron, cooking food, digestion of food, burning paper/wood/candle, passing electricity through water (→ H₂ + O₂), Fe + S heated → FeS)
Alloys — special case (homogeneous mixture)
A
Alloys
Homogeneous mixture of two or more metals (or metal + non-metal). Cannot be separated by physical methods — yet treated as mixture because properties of constituents are retained and composition is variable.
Brass = ~80% Cu + 20% Zn Bronze = ~80% Cu + 20% Sn Stainless steel = Fe + C + Cr + Ni + Mo Solder = Pb + Sn
Exam tips
!
Key points to remember
Homogeneous mixture can have variable composition
Air = homogeneous mixture: N₂ 78%, O₂ 21%, Ar 0.9%. Not a compound.
Tyndall effect test to identify colloid — milk shows it, copper sulphate solution does not
Paperfuge — hand-powered centrifuge from cardboard disc; detects malaria/anaemia in remote areas
Pure substance has sharp, fixed boiling/melting point. Mixture does not.
Crystallisation preferred over evaporation when sugar/substance may char on direct heating, or impurities remain in solution.
ORS (Oral Rehydration Solution) — exact concentration critical; too little/too much pesticide damages crops — concentration matters in real life.
Blood = colloid (not suspension) — cells invisible to naked eye; separated by centrifugation into plasma, platelets, WBC, RBC.
