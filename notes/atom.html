01 · The Big Question

An idea 2,000 years in the making

Long before microscopes existed, thinkers in ancient India and Greece asked the same question. Acharya Kanada imagined splitting matter (dravya) again and again until you hit something that could never be divided — a parmanu. Across the world, Leucippus and Democritus called their version atomos — Greek for "uncuttable." Neither group ran an experiment. It was pure imagination — and they were remarkably close to right.

It took until 1808 for John Dalton to turn the idea into science: atoms, he said, are real, indivisible building blocks of matter. That theory kicked off a 200-year detective story to find out what's actually inside the "uncuttable" atom.

03 · The Experiment That Changed Everything

The gold foil surprise

In 1911, Geiger and Marsden fired tiny positive bullets — alpha particles — at gold foil thinner than paper. If Thomson's "plum pudding" model was right, every particle should sail straight through. Press fire and watch what actually happened.

BEAM OF α-PARTICLES →← THIN GOLD FOIL

Most particles zoomed straight through — the atom is mostly empty space. A few bounced back sharply — they hit something tiny, dense, and positively charged: the nucleus. Rutherford said it was like firing a cannonball at tissue paper and having it bounce back.

04 · Meet the Team

Three particles, one atom

Every atom of everything — you, the air, this screen — is built from just three kinds of Lego bricks.

Proton

charge +1 · symbol p⁺

Lives in the nucleus. Its count is the atom's ID card — the atomic number.

Neutron

charge 0 · symbol n⁰

Also in the nucleus. No charge, but nearly the same mass as a proton — and it glues the nucleus together.

Electron

charge −1 · symbol e⁻

Zips around the nucleus in shells. So light it barely adds any mass at all.

06 · Atomic Twins

Same element, or just same size?

Not every atom of an element is identical — and sometimes different elements share a number by pure coincidence. Here's how to tell them apart.

Protium, deuterium, and tritium are all hydrogen — 1 proton each — but they carry 0, 1, and 2 neutrons. Same chemistry, different mass. That's why chlorine's atomic mass (35.5 u) isn't a whole number — it's a weighted average of ³⁵Cl and ³⁷Cl.

Argon, potassium, and calcium have totally different proton counts — they're different elements. But add up their protons + neutrons and every one lands on mass number 40. A coincidence in total nucleons, nothing more.

08 · At a Glance

Everything on one card

  • Atoms are the building blocks of all matter.
  • Thomson: electrons sit inside a positive sphere (plum pudding).
  • Rutherford: a tiny, dense, positive nucleus with mostly empty space around it.
  • Bohr: electrons orbit in fixed energy levels (shells) without losing energy.
  • Shells fill in order K → L → M → N, holding up to 2n² electrons each.
  • Chadwick discovered the neutral neutron in 1932.
  • Atomic number (Z) = number of protons = number of electrons.
  • Mass number (A) = protons + neutrons.
  • Valency = electrons gained, lost, or shared to complete an octet of 8.
  • Isotopes: same Z, different A. Isobars: different Z, same A.
  • An atom with a full outer shell (an octet) is stable and unreactive.
  • The modern model: electrons live in "clouds" of probability, not fixed orbits.
